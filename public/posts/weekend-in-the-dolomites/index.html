A Weekend in the Dolomites: Notes from the Sella Ronda

2026-03-18 travel

The Sella Ronda is a 26-kilometre loop connecting four Dolomite passes. In summer it belongs to the hikers and the cable cars. In early autumn, if you time it right, you get two days of empty switchbacks and air that feels like it has been laundered.

I rode it twice — once clockwise, once counter-clockwise, because the descents are different enough to make both worthwhile and because I had an extra day and no good reason not to.

The Passo Gardena on the counter-clockwise route is the one that stays with you. The road tilts at gradients that force a choice between grinding a small gear and stopping to breathe, and neither option is wrong. At the top the view opens onto a wall of pale limestone that looks like a stage set, slightly too dramatic to be real.

The villages in the valleys below the passes are a mixture of German, Italian, and Ladin signage — the region was Austrian before 1919 and has not entirely made up its mind since. The food reflects this pleasantly: apple strudel and fresh pasta within walking distance of each other, the coffee excellent in both directions.

Tip for timing: aim for the third week of September. The summer crowds are gone, the first snows are still two or three weeks out, and the angle of light on the rock faces in the late afternoon is something that has no accurate description in English or, as far as I can tell, in any other language.
